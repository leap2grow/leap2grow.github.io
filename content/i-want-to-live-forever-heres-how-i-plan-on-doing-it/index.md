    if (hexColor.length === 3) { hexColor = hexColor.split('').map(function (hex) { return hex + hex;}).join(''); }

  // Get contrast color;
  function getColorContrast(hexColor) {
    const rgb = hexToRgb(hexColor);
    let yiq = ((rgb[0] * 299) + (rgb[1] * 587) + (rgb[2] * 114)) / 1000;
    const colorContrast = (yiq >= 128) ? 'hsl(0 0% 0%)' : 'hsl(0 0% 100%)';

    // return
    return colorContrast
  };

  function hexToHsl(hexColor) {
    const rgb = hexToRgb(hexColor);

    // Make r, g, and b fractions of 1
    const r = rgb[0]/255;
    const g = rgb[1]/255;
    const b = rgb[2]/255;

    const max = Math.max(r, g, b), min = Math.min(r, g, b);
    let h, s, l = (max + min)  /  2;  
    if ( max == min ) { h = s = 0; } else {
      let d = max - min;
      s = l > 0.5 ? d / (2 - max - min) : d / (max + min);
      switch(max){
        case r: h = (g - b) / d + (g < b ? 6 : 0); break;
        case g: h = (b - r) / d + 2; break;
        case b: h = (r - g) / d + 4; break;
      }
      h /= 6;
    }

    return [Math.round(h * 360),Math.round(s * 100),Math.round(l * 100)]
  }

      <p>Life extension is an exciting and controversial topic. And one that I feel a deep-rooted responsibility to write about, <em>because I never plan on dying</em>.</p><p>On the one hand, every human being that has ever lived has perished. Emperors and kings, visionaries and inventors — for all of their accomplishments, every single one of them have been similarly reduced to dust in the annals of time.</p><p>But on the other hand, recent advances in biotechnology are bringing us astoundingly close to understanding the <em>exact mechanism</em> by which human beings age and die.</p><p>We’ve only recently discovered the importance of mitochondrial health, telomere extension, and CRISPR — all in just the last thirty years! And thanks to an ever-accelerating rate of technological change, the <em>next</em> twenty years are likely going to be even more exciting.</p><p>Many people have wanted to live forever… but we’re the first generation that has a chance of actually doing so. Not sure what I mean? I’ll explain.</p><h3 id="practical-immortality-101">Practical Immortality 101</h3><p>The basic line of immortality reasoning is as follows (adjust this with you own numbers for a better or bleaker outlook):</p><ul><li>I am in my mid-twenties. Based on my North American lifespan (81), that gives me approximately sixty more years to live.</li><li>Between now and 2083, we will see profound developments in anti-aging technology that will increase our estimated lifespan.</li><li>Due to the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_change?ref=nicksaraev.com#:~:targetText=An%20analysis%20of%20the%20history,sense%20%27intuitive%20linear%27%20view.&targetText=The%20%27returns%2C%27%20such%20as,the%20rate%20of%20exponential%20growth." rel="noopener"><strong>exponential nature of technology</strong></a>, on average, new advancements will occur faster and provide larger benefits over time.</li><li>Eventually, I will live to a point at which technology adds <strong>more than one year of lifespan </strong>for every extra year that I live. At that point, I will actually be getting biologically <em>younger</em> (healthier, more fit) as time wears on.</li><li>Ergo, <strong>practical immortality.</strong></li></ul><p>Put another way: by the time I <em>would</em> have died, technology will allow me to extend my life by a few more years. Then, by the <em>new </em>time I would have died, technology will allow me again to extend my life. Rinse and repeat until the heat-death of the Universe.</p><p>The technical term for this concept is <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longevity_escape_velocity?ref=nicksaraev.com#:~:targetText=In%20the%20life%20extension%20movement,the%20time%20that%20is%20passing.&targetText=Life%20expectancy%20increases%20slightly%20every%20year%20as%20treatment%20strategies%20and%20technologies%20improve." rel="noopener"><strong>longevity escape velocity</strong></a><strong> (LEV)</strong>. It’s a fascinating idea, and one that we owe heavily to Aubrey De Grey and David Gobel, two of the leading advocates for life extension.</p><h3 id="how-to-achieve-longevity-escape-velocity">How to Achieve Longevity Escape Velocity</h3><p>If I want to live forever, the next question becomes <strong>how to achieve longevity escape velocity</strong>.</p><p>And for most of us, the best way is going to be through instituting lifestyle changes that will increase our natural lifespan as much as possible until technology catches up.</p><p>Other, more scientifically-inclined people might consider directly joining the movement by taking up biotechnology and working for labs or the private sector. But personally, that’s not me. As smart as I like to pretend I am, there are many people more intelligent and better suited for the task.</p><p>So we’ll concern ourselves primarily with the first: <strong>lifestyle changes to make you live longer</strong>.</p><p>A note before I go further: not all of these have been scientifically shown to work. A few, like exercise, have. But due to the nature of statistics, hard science on life extension takes decades to accurately assess and carry out.</p><p>Unfortunately, we don’t have decades. We can’t remain indecisive. If you and I want to live forever, to maximize the statistical probability of achieving LEV, we need need to act <em>now</em> based on our (admittedly) limited knowledge.</p><p>The way I see it, even if some of these lifestyle changes <em>don’t</em> contribute to an increased lifespan, they will, at best, have a neutral or only slightly negative effect. Meaning you and I are almost always better off doing more than less.</p><p>So without further ado…</p><h3 id="here%E2%80%99s-everything-i-do-on-a-daily-basis-to-live-forever">Here’s everything I do on a daily basis to live forever:</h3><ul><li><strong>I consume approximately 2000mg high-quality fish oil daily</strong> with breakfast. Of this, the vast majority (75%+) is EPA/DHA. These fatty acids are suggested to <a href="https://www.medicalnewstoday.com/articles/326533.php?ref=nicksaraev.com#1" rel="noopener">decrease cardiovascular disease risk and improve brain health.</a></li><li><strong>Every morning, I apply broad-spectrum 60SPF sunscreen to my face and neck.</strong> <a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/8080949?ref=nicksaraev.com" rel="noopener">Sun damage is the number one cause of both visible wrinkles and skin cancer</a>. My sunscreen is rated for two hours of direct sun exposure — since most of my day is spent indoors or commuting, I usually don’t have to reapply.</li><li><strong>I take 4000 IU of Vitamin D3 every day with breakfast.</strong> I live in a relatively northern latitude (Vancouver, British Columbia) and don’t get enough Vitamin D strictly from sunlight. <a href="https://www.medicalnewstoday.com/articles/322619.php?ref=nicksaraev.com#1" rel="noopener">Vitamin D supplementation has been shown to have a litany of health benefits, including decreased cancer risk, decreased cardiovascular risk, and more.</a></li><li><strong>Every day, I’ll eat at least four servings of vividly colored, nutrient-rich vegetables.</strong> This is mostly for fiber and mineral content (including Vitamin K). <a href="https://www.health.harvard.edu/healthbeat/tips-for-a-longer-life?ref=nicksaraev.com" rel="noopener">A diet high in fibrous veggies has been shown to benefit lifespan.</a></li><li><strong>I minimize my exposure to overly charred food by steaming or pressure-cooking the majority of my meals.</strong> What we commonly refer to as burned food is a byproduct of Maillard reactions, reactions where carbohydrates and amino acids are heated above approximately 140 degrees C. <a href="https://www.cancer.gov/about-cancer/causes-prevention/risk/diet/cooked-meats-fact-sheet?ref=nicksaraev.com" rel="noopener">Several studies suggest an abundance of charred food is linked to lower lifespans</a>. I personally use an Instant Pot, but you can use whatever you like.</li><li><strong>In the winter months (November through January) I use a small humidifier to raise the ambient indoor humidity of my home to approximately 35%</strong>. I am particularly prone to low humidity, and can develop chapped, split skin and a dry cough. I maintain the hypothesis that chronic coughing, sore throats, etc. raise blood levels of cortisol (the stress hormone) and lead to chronic inflammation, <a href="https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0306453012000686?ref=nicksaraev.com" rel="noopener">both of which have been shown to negatively impact lifespan.</a></li><li><strong>I severely limit the amount of processed food (especially meat) that I consume.</strong> I don’t drink soda or eat pre-made frozen meals, and I minimize smoked or processed meat like sausage or bacon. <a href="https://www.medicalnewstoday.com/articles/324423.php?ref=nicksaraev.com#1" rel="noopener">Common consumption of ultraprocessed foods significantly increases mortality risk.</a></li><li><strong>Every morning, I’ll start my day with a warm cup of green tea</strong>. Green tea is high in epigallocatechin gallate (EPGC), which has been shown to offer myriad health benefits <a href="https://www.lifeextension.com/magazine/2008/4/new-research-on-the-health-benefits-of-green-tea?ref=nicksaraev.com#:~:targetText=The%20largest%20population%2Dbased%20study,coronary%20heart%20disease%2C%20or%20cancer." rel="noopener">like decreased cardiovascular disease risk, decreased cancer risk, and more.</a></li><li><strong>Once every week or so, I’ll have 50–100 grams of 85%+ dark chocolate as a snack.</strong> <a href="http://news.bbc.co.uk/2/hi/health/237652.stm?ref=nicksaraev.com" rel="noopener">Occasional consumption of dark chocolate was correlated with an increased lifespan in a Harvard study.</a></li><li><strong>Every second day, I’ll do 10 minutes of high-intensity cardiovascular exercise</strong>. <a href="https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3395188/?ref=nicksaraev.com#:~:targetText=Aerobic%20endurance%20sports%20resulted%20on,that%20for%20normal%20physical%20activity." rel="noopener">A lifestyle rich in cardiovascular exercise has been suggested to increase lifespan by as much as 4.5–8 years</a>. In all honesty, I could do more cardiovascular exercise, but I <em>strongly </em>dislike it. I also know myself well enough to say that if I added any more, I would probably get sick of it and quit altogether — and I would prefer small consistency over massive variability.</li><li><strong>I weight train three days per week.</strong> My line of reasoning is a better body equates to a better self image, which leads to less cortisol and systemic stress. <a href="http://both%20of%20which%20have%20been%20shown%20to%20negatively%20impact%20lifespan./" rel="noopener">A reduction in cortisol levels leads to a longer lifespan.</a></li></ul><p>People ask me why I don’t want to die. I always found that peculiar — does anybody really <em>want </em>to die? Shouldn’t I be asking you why <em>you don’t want to live</em>?</p><p>We’ve been told our entire lives that death is natural and unavoidable, and to welcome it with open arms. But the older I get, the less I buy into that premise. Nowadays, when people tell me death is a part of life, all I ever say is “know what else used to be a part of life? Smallpox. Not anymore!”</p><p>Life is filled with too much joy and heartbreak — too many profound experiences — to be content with merely letting it slip through our fingers.</p><p>Not to mention the fact that we’ve only experienced a small slice of a fraction of the Universe’s entirety! All of civilization so far has existed on a tiny pale blue dot, winking at eternity. When people say they’ve experienced all that life has to offer, how could that be anything other than incredibly naive?</p><p>What about the next thousand years of human exploration and conquest? What about life on other stars? How would it feel to set foot on a different planet feel, or meet another intelligent species?           navItems[index - 1].classList.add('is-mainitem');
